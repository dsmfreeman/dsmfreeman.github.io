Day 3 · Wednesday, April 8
Inokashira Park + Kichijoji + TeamLab
Early start to Inokashira Park, Where is a Dog? lunch, cat café 2pm, Rizlabo pancakes for dinner, TeamLab by monorail.
9:00 am — Early start
🌸 Inokashira Park — Kichijoji
One of Tokyo's best city parks — a large lake surrounded by cherry trees, swan pedal boats, and a small zoo. In early April the cherry blossoms are either peaking or just past, with petals drifting onto the water. Arrive early to beat the crowds and catch the light. Walk the full lake loop (~30 min), or rent swan boats. The park connects directly to Kichijoji's shopping streets, making it a perfect lead-in to Where is a Dog? when it opens at 11am. Take the Chuo Line from Shinjuku to Kichijoji (~15 min) or from Ginza via Chuo (~30 min).

Day 4 · Thursday, April 9
Cat Train + Cat Temple + Tempura
Ride the Setagaya Line tram to Gōtokuji lucky cat temple, back in time for 6pm tempura dinner in Asakusa.
After breakfast
🐱 Setagaya Line — The Cat Train
From Ginza take the Denentoshi Line to Sangenjaya (~25 min), then transfer to the Setagaya Line — a tiny two-car street tram that trundles through residential Tokyo. Ride to Miyanosaka Station, then walk ~15 min to Gōtokuji Temple. The Setagaya Line sometimes runs a special cat-themed livery; even when it doesn't, the tram itself is a charming transport experience through an unhurried part of the city.

Denentoshi Line from Shibuya to Sangenjaya (2 stops), then Setagaya Line to Miyanosaka. ~40 min total from Ginza.

🐱 Gōtokuji Temple — The Lucky Cat Temple
The birthplace of the Maneki-neko (beckoning cat) — hundreds of white ceramic lucky cats line the temple shelves, left by visitors whose wishes were fulfilled. Free entry, open from 6am. Peaceful, un-touristy, and unlike anywhere else in Tokyo. You can buy a small lucky cat to leave or take home. A 15-min walk from Miyanosaka Station through quiet residential streets with small shops along the way.

Kappabashi + 🎂 Birthday Lunch + ⚾ Baseball
Happy birthday Phoebe! Kappabashi morning, Shochikuen café, birthday lunch at Fleur de Sarrasin, evening at Tokyo Dome.
10:00 – 11:30 am
🔪 Kappabashi Kitchen Street
Tokyo's legendary restaurant supply district — ~170 shops along an 800-metre street between Ueno and Asakusa. Browse: professional Japanese knives (Kama-Asa is the standout shop), beautiful ceramics at bargain prices, baking molds, bamboo steamers, and the hyper-realistic plastic food samples (sampuru) that you see in restaurant windows all over Japan. Affordable keychain and magnet versions make great souvenirs. Most shops open from 10am. Note: most are closed Sundays — Friday is ideal.

⚾ Yomiuri Giants at Tokyo Dome
Japanese baseball culture is unlike anything in the US: synchronized fan chants section by section, umbrella dances in the 7th inning, mid-game fireworks, beer keg ladies who pour at your seat, and bento boxes everywhere. The Giants are Japan's most famous team. Don't worry about not knowing the chants — it's infectious, you'll pick them up.

🏛 WWII Museums — Japanese Perspective
Two museums near Kudanshita Station, 20 min from Ginza. Start at the National Showa Memorial Museum (Showakan) — focuses on the daily lives of ordinary Japanese civilians during and after the war: rationing, air raids, personal letters, rebuilt lives. Fascinating and human. ¥300 entry. Download the Pocket Curator app for English translations. Then walk 5 min to Yushukan inside Yasukuni Shrine — Japan's oldest military museum with a Zero fighter aircraft, kamikaze pilot letters, and a naval locomotive from the Death Railway. The narrative frames Japan as reluctant and defensive, which is controversial but illuminating to experience having just been at Pearl Harbor. ¥1,000 entry. Together allow 2–2.5 hours.
