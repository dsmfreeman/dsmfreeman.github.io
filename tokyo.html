🎮 Akihabara — Super Potato + GiGO Arcade
Take the Hibiya Line from Meiji-Jingumae to Akihabara (20 min direct). Head straight to Super Potato: three floors of retro games and consoles in a building painted in Mario motifs, plus a working retro arcade on the 5th floor with original 80s/90s cabinets — Street Fighter, Pac-Man, Donkey Kong. Japanese versions of familiar games, rare Famicom hardware, original Game Boy units. Even if you don't buy, it's a genuine museum of gaming history. Then explore GiGO Akihabara's RETRO:G on the 6th floor for more arcade action. The broader Akihabara street can be overwhelming — stick to these two anchors and browse organically.

10:00 – 11:30 am
🔪 Kappabashi Kitchen Street
Tokyo's legendary restaurant supply district — ~170 shops along an 800-metre street between Ueno and Asakusa. Browse: professional Japanese knives (Kama-Asa is the standout shop), beautiful ceramics at bargain prices, baking molds, bamboo steamers, and the hyper-realistic plastic food samples (sampuru) that you see in restaurant windows all over Japan. Affordable keychain and magnet versions make great souvenirs. Most shops open from 10am. Note: most are closed Sundays — Friday is ideal.
shopping · kitchen supplies
📍 Kappabashi Kitchen Street
Start at the giant chef's head statue (Niimi shop corner) and walk north. Side streets are worth exploring. Most shops close by 5–6pm.
11:45 am — BOOK AHEAD
🥐 Fleur de Sarrasin — Birthday Lunch 🎂
A 10-min walk from Kappabashi into Nishi-Asakusa. Opens Friday lunch at 11:45am. Husband and wife team who lived in France — buckwheat ground fresh daily for the galettes (crepes). 100% GF. The duck galette and camembert + walnut + honey galette are standouts. Tiny, intimate, and genuinely exceptional — the perfect birthday setting. Tell them it's a birthday when you book.

⚾ Yomiuri Giants at Tokyo Dome
Japanese baseball culture is unlike anything in the US: synchronized fan chants section by section, umbrella dances in the 7th inning, mid-game fireworks, beer keg ladies who pour at your seat, and bento boxes everywhere. The Giants are Japan's most famous team. Don't worry about not knowing the chants — it's infectious, you'll pick them up.

Day 6 · Saturday, April 11
WWII Museums + Depart
History morning near Kudanshita, farewell dinner in Ginza, Keikyu to Haneda for the 10:55pm flight.
9:30 – 12:00 pm
🏛 WWII Museums — Japanese Perspective
Two museums near Kudanshita Station, 20 min from Ginza. Start at the National Showa Memorial Museum (Showakan) — focuses on the daily lives of ordinary Japanese civilians during and after the war: rationing, air raids, personal letters, rebuilt lives. Fascinating and human. ¥300 entry. Download the Pocket Curator app for English translations. Then walk 5 min to Yushukan inside Yasukuni Shrine — Japan's oldest military museum with a Zero fighter aircraft, kamikaze pilot letters, and a naval locomotive from the Death Railway. The narrative frames Japan as reluctant and defensive, which is controversial but illuminating to experience having just been at Pearl Harbor. ¥1,000 entry. Together allow 2–2.5 hours.
history · WWII
📍 Showakan (National Showa Memorial Museum) 📍 Yushukan (Yasukuni Shrine)
Showakan: 10am–5:30pm, open Saturdays. Yushukan: 9am–4pm, open Saturdays. Both walkable from Kudanshita Station (Hanzomon/Tozai/Toei Shinjuku lines). Download the Pocket Curator app before visiting Showakan for English translations.
Optional detour
Comme'N GF Bakery — Setagaya
~20 min from Kichijoji by train. Their GF baguette is reportedly the best celiac baguette in Japan: genuinely crusty outside, pillowy inside. Small park across the street to sit and eat. Worth it if you're bread lovers — skip if you'd rather head back and relax before the airport run.

🍽 Farewell Dinner — Gonpachi Asakusa
A casual izakaya with a dedicated GF menu — GF tempura, yakitori skewers off the charcoal grill, sashimi, karaage chicken. The kind of lively, smoky, order-everything Japanese pub dinner that's a perfect last meal. English menu and GF menu both available. Sits along the Sumida River with a nice view. About 25 min from the hotel by train (Toei Asakusa Line from Higashi-Ginza to Asakusa, 2 stops), or taxi. Conveniently, the same Toei Asakusa Line continues directly to the Keikyu Line for Haneda — so you can go Ginza → Gonpachi → airport without backtracking.

🔪 Kappabashi Kitchen Street
Tokyo's legendary restaurant supply district — ~170 shops along an 800-metre street between Ueno and Asakusa. Browse: professional Japanese knives (Kama-Asa is the standout shop), beautiful ceramics at bargain prices, baking molds, bamboo steamers, and the hyper-realistic plastic food samples (sampuru) that you see in restaurant windows all over Japan. Affordable keychain and magnet versions make great souvenirs. Most shops open from 10am. Note: most are closed Sundays — Friday is ideal.

WWII Museums + Depart
History morning near Kudanshita, farewell dinner in Ginza, Keikyu to Haneda for the 10:55pm flight.
9:30 – 12:00 pm
🏛 WWII Museums — Japanese Perspective
Two museums near Kudanshita Station, 20 min from Ginza. Start at the National Showa Memorial Museum (Showakan) — focuses on the daily lives of ordinary Japanese civilians during and after the war: rationing, air raids, personal letters, rebuilt lives. Fascinating and human. ¥300 entry. Download the Pocket Curator app for English translations. Then walk 5 min to Yushukan inside Yasukuni Shrine — Japan's oldest military museum with a Zero fighter aircraft, kamikaze pilot letters, and a naval locomotive from the Death Railway. The narrative frames Japan as reluctant and defensive, which is controversial but illuminating to experience having just been at Pearl Harbor. ¥1,000 entry. Together allow 2–2.5 hours.
history · WWII
